Key Formulas
Sharpe Ratio
[E(R) − Rf] ÷ σ
where Rf = 4.2% · Higher = better risk-adjusted return
CAPM / SML
Rf + β × [E(Rm) − Rf]
= 4.2% + β × 6.3% · Above SML = alpha (favorable)
Duration Rate Risk
Duration × ΔRate × $500M
Group B: 8.5 × 1% × $500M = $42.5M loss
Debt Carry Spread
E(R) − 5.0% (debt coupon)
Negative = losing money vs. paying down debt

Key Teaching Insight: None of the five strategies is obviously correct. Each is defensible on at least one dimension and fatally flawed on at least one other. Group E — concentrated stock picking — forces the sharpest Chapter 11 conversation: unsystematic risk is real, it is uncompensated, and a corporate CFO has no business holding it.

CFO Attack Lines
Hardball Q&A
Universal questions every group faces — plus targeted attacks by group.
Universal — All Groups Face These
"Walk me through the carry trade: we're paying 5% on $2 billion of debt. What is your strategy earning net of that cost?"
"I have to present this to our audit committee. They approved an IPS. Your strategy either violates it or barely fits it. Which is it, and what do I tell them?"
"We expect to need $300–400M for an acquisition in about 18 months. If I put all $500M into your strategy today, can I get that much back without taking a loss?"
"We trade at 6× revenue. Our investors own us because they believe in our growth story. What does parking $500M in your strategy say about how we think about the future of this company?"
"What is the single biggest counterparty risk in your strategy — and what is the worst-case outcome if that counterparty fails?"
"If I do nothing — just leave the cash in a bank account — what does that cost me relative to your strategy? Make the case for why moving the money at all is worth the complexity."
Group-Specific Attacks
CFO Wild Cards
Curveball Scenarios
CFO may fire one mid-class. Prepare a 30–90 second response.
⚡ Curveball 1 — Duration Trap
The board just approved a $350M acquisition. The deal could close in 6 months. Group B — you told us to hold the 10-year Treasury to maturity. The 10-year yield has risen 80bps since you bought. What is the mark-to-market loss if you sell today? Use duration 8.5 years. You have 90 seconds.
Answer framework
MTM Loss = 8.5 × 0.80% × $500M = $34M. Group B must confront the contradiction: "hold to maturity" and "fund M&A in 6 months" cannot both be true.
⚡ Curveball 2 — IPS Violation
Our audit committee just reviewed the investment portfolio. Group D — they flagged your equity ETF as an IPS violation. They want to know why the CFO approved an equity position when the policy explicitly prohibits it. You have 60 seconds to make the case for a retroactive policy exception, or tell me how we unwind without a loss.
Answer framework
Group D must either defend a governance process (board vote, amendment timeline) or admit unwind risk: selling $500M of ETF at market price with no guarantee of timing.
⚡ Curveball 3 — Counterparty Event
One of Group A's eight corporate bond issuers just had their credit downgraded from BBB+ to BB− — below investment grade. The IPS requires investment-grade only. You must sell that tranche immediately. Credit spreads have widened 150bps. What is the approximate loss, and how does this change your pitch?
Answer framework
~$62.5M tranche × duration ~1yr × 1.5% spread widen ≈ $940K loss plus bid-ask. Forces the "fallen angel" BBB risk discussion Group A's thesis depends on avoiding.
⚡ Curveball 4 — Rate Shock
The Fed just cut rates 100bps. Every group — update your carry analysis. What are you now earning relative to our 5% fixed-rate debt? Which strategy looks best and worst on a net carry basis? You have 90 seconds each.
Answer framework
T-bills (C) drop to ~3.2% → carry becomes −1.8%. Group A bonds adjust slightly. Groups D & E equity returns unchanged by rate cuts. This rewards the fixed-coupon bond strategies on carry.
⚡ Curveball 5 — Activist Shareholder
An activist fund just filed a 13D. They want us to return all $500M to shareholders via buyback at our current price. Every group — you have 60 seconds to argue that your investment strategy creates more value per share than a buyback, given that we trade at 6× revenue.
Answer framework
Must calculate buyback share reduction vs. investment return, and argue why M&A optionality or carry exceeds EPS accretion from repurchase at a 6× revenue multiple.
⚡ Curveball 6 — Earnings Shock (Group E)
One of Group E's five stocks just missed earnings by 40% and is down 35% pre-market. Their $100M position is now worth $65M — a $35M realized loss if they sell. The board wants to know why we own a single company's stock in our corporate treasury. Group E: you have 90 seconds to defend the position or recommend we cut the loss.
Answer framework
This is the Chapter 11 lesson made real: $35M idiosyncratic loss from one earnings miss. CAPM says this risk earns zero premium. Group E must defend why they accepted uncompensated risk.
🔀 Split Budget Twist — Use Anytime
"I've decided to split the $500M — $300M must be ready for M&A within 18 months in maximum-liquidity form, and $200M can be invested for return over 2 years. Revise your pitches. Which tranche are you pitching for, and does your strategy survive the split?"
Answer framework
Forces every group to decide: liquidity tranche or return tranche? Group C is natural for $300M. Group A 2-yr rungs for $200M. Groups D/E face impossible liquidity constraint on the $300M tranche.

Final 10 Minutes
Debrief Discussion
Surface the gap between textbook theory and real CFO decisions.
Question 1
Group B's Treasury has zero counterparty risk but maximum duration risk and negative carry against our debt. Group C has zero duration risk but negative carry and destroys growth-company optics. Which is the lesser evil — and why does that depend on the company's specific situation?
Question 2
The IPS was written to protect the company. Three groups violated it. Was there a version of any of those pitches where the board should have said yes anyway? What would it take?
Question 3
We're paying 5% on $2B of debt. Three of the five strategies earn less than 5.8%. What does that tell you about the real purpose of holding this cash — it's not about return, so what is it about?
Question 4
Group E picked five individual stocks. Group D bought 500. Both violated the IPS. But Chapter 11 says unsystematic risk is uncompensated. Which equity group made the worse mistake — and does the chapter theory actually answer that question?
Question 5
Group E had the highest E(R) at 14% and the highest σ at 28%. Their Sharpe ratio is (14%−4.2%)/28% = 0.350 — lower than Group D's 0.450. What does that tell us about the cost of underdiversification?
Question 6
The "10-year bond held to maturity" framing is very seductive — you get the full yield. What is the hidden assumption that makes that framing dangerous for a company with active M&A plans?
Question 7
What would the guest CFO actually do with $500M right now — and how closely does that match any of the five strategies they just heard pitched?
Closing Teaching Moment: Textbook portfolio theory optimizes return per unit of risk for a rational investor with a stable, long-horizon mandate. A corporate CFO is optimizing across five different constraint sets simultaneously: IPS compliance, board governance, analyst optics, M&A optionality, and debt service. The math is the same. The mandate is completely different.
